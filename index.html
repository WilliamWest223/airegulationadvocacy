01

Workforce Disruption

Work, rewritten without consent.

The first wave of generative AI was sold as augmentation. The reality arriving in hiring pipelines, customer-service floors, and white-collar offices looks more like replacement — and, where humans remain, surveillance. The question is no longer whether work will be automated, but who gets to decide the terms.

300M

full-time jobs globally exposed to automation by generative AI.

Economists estimate that roughly two-thirds of occupations face some degree of automation from current AI systems — a disruption arriving faster than reskilling programs, social safety nets, or labor law can absorb.

Goldman Sachs Global Economics, 2023

Amazon.

An internal AI recruiter penalized résumés containing the word "women's."

Trained on a decade of male-dominated hiring data, the system learned that maleness correlated with being hired — and encoded that into its scoring. The tool was scrapped in 2018, but the pattern is not unique to Amazon. Without regulation, historical discrimination is laundered through code and rebranded as objectivity.

Reuters investigation, 2018

Automation is not inherently unjust. Unregulated automation, administered by private firms with no obligation to the workers it displaces, is.

02

The Educational Dilemma

Learning requires friction.

Universities have responded to AI in schools the way institutions always respond to new technology: with panic, prohibitions, and detection software. The more durable problem is harder to name. When a student can outsource the struggle of thinking, the cognitive development that is the education doesn't happen at all.

“

An AI that removes the friction of learning also removes the learning. What remains is a credential — a certificate that someone, or something, answered the questions.

Claimed

98%

accuracy, per Turnitin's marketing materials for its AI-detection product at launch.

In practice

2×

the rate at which non-native English writing is wrongly flagged as AI-generated, per Stanford researchers.

Detection tools offer universities the appearance of a solution while shifting the cost of their errors onto the students least equipped to contest them. The honest response is harder: redesign the assignment, redesign the incentive, and accept that no software will do the institution's thinking for it.

03

Societal Risks

A public sphere, privately owned.

Two trends are converging. The cost of producing persuasive synthetic content has collapsed to near-zero, and the infrastructure on which that content travels is concentrated in fewer hands than at any point in modern history. Democratic discourse is being asked to function on a substrate it was not designed for.

Misinformation

Synthetic speech at industrial scale.

In January 2024, voters in New Hampshire received robocalls in a synthesized voice urging them not to vote in the primary. The audio cost roughly a dollar to generate. Elections officials traced the source; most such operations will never be traced. The bottleneck on political deception used to be production — it is now distribution, and distribution belongs to a handful of platforms with no statutory duty to police it.

Concentrated Power

Three firms, one stack.

The compute, the data, and the frontier models that underwrite contemporary AI sit inside a small set of corporations. When a single company can decide what its model will say about an election, a disease, or a war — and when that company is accountable only to its shareholders — the line between a private product decision and a public policy act has already been crossed.

~65%

Approximate share of frontier-model compute and capital held by the three largest AI developers.

04

The Turn

What regulation actually looks like.

"Regulation" is often invoked as a vague imposition — bureaucratic, innovation-killing, un-American. In practice, the serious proposals are narrower and more concrete than that. Three principles, borrowed from decades of policy work in aviation, medicine, and finance, are sufficient to ground the rest.

I

Mandatory Transparency

Any AI system deployed at scale must publicly document what it was trained on, how it was evaluated, and where it is known to fail. No proprietary-secrecy exemption for systems that act on the public.

Analogue nutrition labels; drug trial disclosure; SEC filings.

II

Human-in-the-Loop

Consequential decisions — hiring, lending, medical diagnosis, sentencing, immigration — may use AI as an input, but the decision of record must rest with a named human who can be questioned, overruled, and held accountable.

Analogue a pilot who can override the autopilot.

III

A Right to Explanation

When an algorithm denies you a job, a loan, or a benefit, you are entitled to a specific, human-readable reason — not a confidence score. Without it, there is no appeal. Without appeal, there is no law.

Analogue the adverse-action notice required in U.S. credit decisions.

The horizon is not distant. It is here, and it is being drawn for us.
